The Child Who Woke the Stars

by Kevyn Patrick Malpass
Dedicated to my Mom and Dad, who taught me to be myself, no matter what others thought of me.
Listen

A very long time ago, before the sky learned how to shimmer and sparkle, the nights were whole and unbroken.

Each night, after the Sun had set beyond the horizon, darkness covered the Earth like a deep ocean. The only source of light in the sky was the Moon. It drifted overhead – pale, patient, and alone.

The Moon gave what light it could, pouring silver across rooftops, rivers, and sleeping faces. It never failed its post.

But the Moons light was borrowed. It reflected what it was given by the Sun. Its light fell upon the world thin and narrow, like a single thought in a stubborn mind. When the Moon turned its face away, or stayed below the horizon, the sky gave only darkness.

The world did not know these details. The people simply said, "See? We have moonlight. This is how it is. "

And so, the night sky remained singular.

In a small town beneath that solitary glow, there lived a child who was different from the other kids they knew. They had a softness in their heart where others were hard, a tenderness that leaned in unexpected directions. Their difference was small in the measure of the cosmos.

But the other kids noticed.

They sensed it the way some animals sense bad weather. Children have a way of detecting the faintest oddity about a person and calling attention to it. They circled like restless birds. They laughed at what they could not name. They feared what they did not understand. And because they did not understand, they decided the kid must be wrong.

The child did not know what made them different. They only knew that when they laughed, it sounded wrong to others. When they loved, others ridiculed them and said their love was wrong. No one could fully explain why they were wrong. The people, young and old alike, tormented this child with their teasing, mocking, and sometimes outright cruelty

This brought great harm to the child, and so they learned.

They studied the other kids closely. They memorized their jokes and practiced their posture. They altered their voice and trimmed their words before speaking. They dressed in clothing they disliked. They borrowed the mannerisms of the others like the Moon borrowed light, reflecting what was handed to them, until they were polished smooth, pale, and acceptable.

They became very good at it.

The other children noticed. They stopped laughing. The teasing thinned. The torment faded. The world, satisfied with this, turned its gaze elsewhere.

"See, " they said. "Just a normal child after all. "

But the child felt something falter inside their chest. There had always been a warmth there– a glow that did not come from outside. They had felt it ever since they were very young. It was a warm pulse beneath their ribs, like a tiny sunrise trying to happen.

Every time they swallowed the truth, that light dimmed. Each time they nodded at a lie, the warmth faded. Whenever they pretended to be other than they were, their inner glow flickered, threatening to go out completely.

One night, when the Moon had not yet risen, the child lay awake, pressing their palm against their chest. They could no longer feel the warmth inside.

Instead, they felt only silence and darkness, mirroring the world outside when the Moon's light was not overhead.

For the first time, they realized that the darkness outside was not unlike the darkness that people carried within themselves. People were afraid.

Afraid of what was unfamiliar.

Afraid of colors they had not yet learned to name.

Afraid of light that did not come from where they expected.

They thought of the sky, vast and empty except for that lonely moon. At night, all light came from one place, one direction. One approved shape. No wonder people feared anything else.

"What if, " they whispered into the dark, "the night had more lights? "

They imagined many points of brightness scattered across the sky, each one shining separately, from its own direction. No single light dominating. No single way of being illuminated.

The child felt something inside of them shift. They could feel their chest warming, their glow growing brighter.

They stopped pretending the very next morning.

It was not dramatic. There were no speeches. They simply ceased bending toward the expectations of others.

When the other kids gathered to tell their crude jokes, they did not force a laugh. When the others spoke of future wants and desires, they did not pretend to share those dreams. When cruel kids mocked someone new for being different, the child walked away and befriended the newcomer, glowing softly from within.

The teasing returned, sharper this time. The cruelty intensified.

"What happened to you?" one boy asked. "You were almost one of us."

The child looked at them, really looked at them, and saw not enemies but frightened children who had been taught that there was only one right way to be. Only one approved kind of light.

"I am tired of borrowing light, " they said quietly. "I have my own. "

The boy did not understand. He could not understand. The others redoubled their efforts to extinguish the child's glow, to bring them back into the familiar darkness where everyone looked the same.

But the child's light only grew stronger. Each time the world tried to dim them, they shined brighter. Each time others tried to make them feel small, they expanded. Each time other people, young and old alike, tried to force the child back into their shape, this child became more distinctly their own self.

The child's inner glow, no longer smothered, grew fierce. It poured through their skin. It gathered in their eyes. It trembled in their voice.

People shielded their faces when the child walked by, not because they were burned, but because the child revealed them.

Though the child had newfound confidence, they were still afraid of being harmed because they were different.

Courage is not the absence of fear.

It is choosing not to extinguish yourself because of it.

The next night, the child climbed to the top of the highest hill. The world below was inky black. The Moon hung low, luminous and solitary.

"Why do you shine alone? " the child asked softly.

The Moon regarded them.

"I do not shine, " it said after a long silence. "I reflect. It is my duty. "

The child frowned. "Does it make you happy? "

The Moon's light trembled almost imperceptibly. "It keeps the dark away, " it said. "The world relies on me. "

"But aren't you lonely? " the child asked. "What if there were other lights up there, to keep you company? "

The Moon did not answer at once.

"I didn't choose to be alone, " the Moon whispered. "I didn't choose the light I reflect. But I do what I can. I fear that if there were more lights, I would no longer be needed. "

The child understood that fear. They had lived it.

"I tried to be like everyone else," they said. "I thought if I reflected them well enough, I would stop hurting."

"And did you?" asked the Moon.

"No," they said. "It only made me smaller."

Silence stretched between them, vast and honest.

"What if," the child said carefully, "there were other lights? Not just one or two. But many. And not to replace you. Not to outshine you. But to share the sky with you."

The Moon dimmed slightly, thinking.

"The world is afraid of what shines differently," it said.

"So was I," the child replied.

"And what did you do?"

They drew a steady breath.

“I stopped hiding.”

They closed their eyes, letting every part of their being exist at once, bright, tender, and unashamed. The light within them flared. It rose from their chest like a breath they had been holding their entire life. It spilled upward, past treetops, past clouds, into the great waiting sky.

The universe noticed.

It had been watching all along, as universes do, curious about small, stubborn flames that refuse to die. It peered into the child's heart and found no hunger for dominance. No wish to erase what came before. Only a longing for others to see from more than one direction. To live without shrinking.

To wake up and understand.

The universe is vast and ancient, but it is not unkind. It took the light the child offered and did not keep it.

Instead, it scattered the light across the sky. Pinpricks became lanterns. Lanterns became constellations. Light bloomed in every direction. Each star burned with the same essence, fundamentally alike, yet each distinct in position and brightness. They shined from every direction, so no one could claim light belonged to only one source.

In the village below, people stumbled from their homes. They stared upward; faces washed in new radiance. Shadows no longer fell in a single direction; they crossed and softened. Corners once hidden revealed their shape.

And the Moon? As it rose above the horizon, it looked around at the countless new lights in the sky, and for the first time, no longer felt alone.

On the hill, the child fell to their knees, breathless.

They had not meant to remake the heavens.

They had only meant to remain themselves.

But sometimes that is enough.

The world was transformed after that night. The sky was no longer a dark expanse lit by a lone, borrowed glow; it had become a canvas of infinite variety. Each star glowed according to its own nature, contributing its unique spark to the beauty of the whole. The Moon continued its dance across the heavens against a backdrop of a thousand twinkling lights, no longer alone.

The child grew into an adult who continued to shine their light without apology or compromise. Others, seeing their courage, began to find their own light within themselves. Some glowed brightly, some softly, some in colors never before seen. But all were beautiful. All were necessary.

The darkness never completely disappeared. There were still those who preferred the old way, the single light, the familiar shapes. But they were no longer the only voice. The night sky itself testified that there were infinite ways to be, infinite ways to love, infinite ways to shine.

When a child feels different or alone, they need only look up at the night sky to remember: every star they see shines because someone chose to share their own inner glow, making it safer for others to do the same.

Darkness is not defeated by sameness.

It is transformed by many brave lights shining at once.

And that is how a child woke the stars. ✨🖖
